About — Design Philosophy

A practice rooted in how people actually learn.

My work begins with one question: what is preventing the desired performance? Knowledge gaps are only one possible answer. Often the real cause is environment, incentives, communication, or cognitive load — and a training course is not the solution.

When learning is the answer, I design it to do real work. That means experiences that respect how attention, memory, and motivation function — and that meet learners where they are, not where the brief assumes they should be.

"Good learning design is not decoration around content. It is the architecture of a behaviour change."

Instructional Design Storyboard

Leading Healthy Teams

Modules 1 & 3 — Scenario-Based Microlearning for Corporate L&D

AudienceFirst-line managers, team leaders
Duration~5 min per module (microlearning)
Build ToolArticulate Storyline / Rise 360
FrameworkPerformance analysis-driven
SettingCorporate / IT organisation
Modules3 modules, 10 screens each
Module 1: Recognising Unintentional Burnout Triggers

Help managers identify early burnout indicators, unintentional behaviours, and hidden communication patterns before they escalate.

This module is the diagnostic foundation of the course. Managers enter believing their team is fine and leave understanding that burnout signals start long before performance drops — and that their own communication patterns are often the unexamined variable. The module moves from emotional disruption through analytical tools to personal calibration. It deliberately avoids prescribing solutions; that is Module 2's job.

Narrative thread: Rajesh Mehta, the composite IT manager persona, appears throughout. His team members (Sneha, Amit, Karan, Priya) build character continuity across all three modules.
Emotional arc: Discomfort → Recognition → Analysis → Calibration → Commitment
M1-01 The Invisible Problem ▾
Learning Purpose

Hook the learner with a recognisable scenario that disrupts the assumption that burnout is always visible. Establish the module's premise: manager behaviours are often the unexamined variable.

On-Screen Content

A simulated Slack-style notification panel showing three messages in sequence — Sneha marking herself 'Away' for the third consecutive week; Amit's project update four days late; Karan requesting a transfer. Below: "You are Rajesh. You manage this team. None of them have complained. What are you missing?"

InteractionAuto-reveal sequence → Continue
Developer Notes

Notification animation should feel native to corporate messaging tools (Slack/Teams aesthetic). Use subtle slide-in from right. Storyline: timeline-based animation layers. Rise 360: process block with sequential reveal.

M1-02 The Burnout Iceberg ▾
Learning Purpose

Distinguish between visible burnout symptoms (what managers notice too late) and invisible early indicators (what they should be watching for). Build the cognitive frame for the rest of the module.

On-Screen Content

An iceberg diagram with two zones. Above waterline (visible): missed deadlines, absenteeism, performance drops, resignations. Below waterline (invisible): declining meeting participation, shorter messages, stopped volunteering, false compliance, emotional withdrawal.

InteractionClick-to-reveal — below-waterline items
Feedback

After all items revealed: research note citing Maslach & Leiter (2016) — emotional exhaustion is measurable months before performance declines.

M1-03 The Mirror ▾
Learning Purpose

Force self-recognition. Present five common manager behaviours framed positively and reveal the burnout cost of each. Goal: calibration, not guilt.

On-Screen Content

Five flip cards. Fronts: positive leadership framings ("I keep my team in the loop by CC-ing them"; "I'm always available even after hours"). Backs: the reframe showing impact on Sneha, Amit, Karan, and Priya specifically.

InteractionClick-to-flip — all 5 must be flipped
Feedback

After all cards flipped: "Pause. Which of these felt familiar? This module is not about blame — it's about seeing the gap between intent and impact."

M1-04 Communication Pattern Audit ▾
Learning Purpose

Move from awareness to analysis. Learners sort real communication behaviours into Sustainable vs Burnout-Driving categories, calibrating against research-backed criteria.

On-Screen Content

8 items to sort. Key examples: sending a Slack at 11 PM with "no need to respond tonight" (burnout-driving — the message resets the norm regardless of caveat); scheduling focus time on team calendar (sustainable); praising the team member who responded fastest to a weekend request (burnout-driving).

InteractionDrag-and-drop sorting → Check Answers
Feedback

Items 1, 5, and 7 have extended callouts (most commonly misjudged). Score tiers: 7–8 strong pattern recognition; 4–6 some genuinely ambiguous; 0–3 shows exactly why this module exists.

M1-05 The Availability Trap ▾
Learning Purpose

Apply learning to a realistic scenario. Show how a single after-hours communication choice cascades through the team. Demonstrate that intent does not control impact.

Scenario

9:15 PM. Rajesh spots an error on a client deck due tomorrow. Sneha built it and is the only one who can fix it quickly. Three options: message now; message with "no need to look until morning"; set a scheduled 7:30 AM message and fix what you can yourself.

InteractionBranching scenario — single select, locks on choice
Key Learning

Option C consequence: Sneha gets a 7:30 AM message, fixes it in 15 minutes. "You modelled the boundary you want your team to hold." No choice is labelled correct/incorrect — all three are analysed for impact.

M1-06 Reading the Room ▾
Learning Purpose

Shift from individual behaviours to team-level pattern recognition. Train managers to notice systemic warning signs versus normal team dynamics.

Key Design Choice

Includes deliberate "normal" items: two people openly disagreeing in a meeting (healthy conflict, not burnout); a team member declining optional social events (preference, not withdrawal). Item 5 feedback: "Disagreement is not dysfunction. Silence is more dangerous than friction."

InteractionMulti-select checklist → Check Answers
M1-07 The Compounding Effect ▾
Learning Purpose

Show how individually minor behaviours accumulate into a burnout trajectory. Counter the objection: "But it was just one message / one check-in."

On-Screen Content

A Monday–Friday timeline for Rajesh: 4:45 PM meeting scheduled; full-team CC on email; premature status-update request pulling Karan off actual work; 8:55 PM channel message about a demo slide. A running "invisible cost" counter accumulates to 3 hrs 20 min of unplanned work by Friday.

InteractionClick-to-reveal timeline with live cost counter
M1-08 Your Behavioural Fingerprint ▾
Learning Purpose

Transition from Rajesh's story to the learner's own practice. A structured private self-audit mapping their communication patterns against the burnout triggers covered.

On-Screen Content

6 self-assessment statements (e.g. "I send messages outside agreed working hours"; "I schedule meetings in the last 30 minutes of the workday") rated on a Never → Regularly scale. Closing: "Look at your pattern. Where are you closest to Rajesh?"

InteractionPrivate reflection — NOT scored or submitted to LMS
M1-09 Scenario Check ▾
Scenario

Priya is still delivering high-quality work and meeting every deadline — but her Slack status has gone generic, and she ended her last 1:1 five minutes early. She used to run over.

Question

What is the most significant early indicator? Options: (A) She ended the 1:1 early — correct; (B) Generic Slack status; (C) Said everything was fine; (D) No clear indicator — she's still performing.

Key Insight

"She hasn't disengaged from the work yet — she's disengaging from you. That's the earliest and most actionable signal." Single attempt — no retry, the explanation does the teaching.

InteractionSingle-select knowledge check — one attempt only
M1-10 Bridge to Action ▾
Learning Purpose

Close Module 1 and set up Module 2. Summarise the three key shifts and create narrative continuity into the boundary-setting module.

Three Key Shifts

1. Burnout signals start below the waterline — by the time you see performance drops, you're months late. 2. Your intent doesn't control your impact — a 9 PM message with "no rush" still carries urgency. 3. Team-level silence is not team-level health — when everyone agrees and no one pushes back, check whether they've given up.

Developer Notes

Do NOT include a "Module 1 Complete" congratulations screen. The tone of this course is consulting, not gamification. A congratulations message would undercut the gravity of the content.

Module 3: Reinforcing Sustainable Performance

Help managers reinforce healthy norms, model sustainable practices, and build systems that make behaviour change persist beyond training.

This module addresses the most common failure point in corporate learning: the gap between completing a course and changing behaviour. Module 1 built awareness. Module 2 gave tools. Module 3 builds the reinforcement infrastructure that prevents regression.

Narrative thread: Rajesh's story picks up six weeks after Module 2 — his initial improvements are already fading, showing that awareness and tools are necessary but not sufficient without systems.
Emotional arc: Concern → Application → Infrastructure → Pressure test → Commitment
M3-01 The 90-Day Problem ▾
Learning Purpose

Establish why Module 3 exists. Awareness and tools are necessary but not sufficient without reinforcement systems. Frame the challenge as behavioural, not intellectual.

On-Screen Content

A dashboard showing Rajesh's team health metrics six weeks after Module 2. After-hours messages: down 70%, now creeping back to 40%. Team engagement: 3.1 → 4.2, now drifting to 3.8. Boundary agreement adherence: 95% month 1, 72% month 2. The regression IS the feedback mechanism.

InteractionAuto-reveal dashboard → Continue
M3-02 Walking the Talk ▾
Scenario

Rajesh set a Friday no-meeting focus day two months ago. A VP now requests a Friday morning cross-functional review. Three options: accept (it's a VP), propose Monday instead (protecting the team agreement), or make it optional for the team while attending himself.

Key Insight

Option C (attend alone, make it optional) appears considerate but undermines the norm: "If the manager doesn't protect his own focus day, the implicit message is that focus days are for people who can afford them — not for leaders."

InteractionBranching scenario — all 3 consequences revealed for comparison
M3-03 The Recognition Trap ▾
Learning Purpose

Reframe what managers recognise and reward. Most recognition systems inadvertently reward unsustainable behaviours (heroics, overwork, speed) rather than sustainable ones.

Key Item

Item 7: "Priya handled three escalations this sprint — she's a rockstar." → Reinforces Burnout. Feedback: "Three escalations in one sprint means the system is failing. Praising the person who absorbs the failure hides the systemic issue."

InteractionDrag-and-drop sorting — mirrors M1-04 UX pattern
M3-04 Psychological Safety Audit ▾
Learning Purpose

Introduce psychological safety as the infrastructure that makes sustainable performance possible. Without it, boundaries collapse under pressure because no one feels safe enforcing them.

Four Indicators

People disagree openly in meetings; mistakes are reported early not hidden; people ask for help without lengthy qualifiers; feedback flows upward, not just downward. Each indicator has a scenario-based reveal using the recurring team members.

InteractionClick-to-reveal accordion — all 4 must be opened
M3-05 The Sustainability Conversation ▾
Scenario

1:1 with Amit. His work quality is strong, but you've noticed early indicators: shorter messages, fewer questions in meetings. He opens with: "Everything's fine. Let's talk about the migration timeline."

Key Distinction

Option B ("How are things really going?") vs Option C ("I've noticed you've been quieter in standups, and your messages have gotten shorter"): "The difference is specificity. Caring questions land when they're grounded in something observable." Option C reveals Amit is covering for integration gaps he hasn't flagged.

InteractionConversation simulation — all 3 results revealed for comparison
M3-06 Building Team Agreements That Last ▾
Learning Purpose

Teach the difference between norms imposed by the manager and norms co-created with the team — and why only the latter sustain under pressure.

Key Contrast

"No Slack messages after 6 PM" (imposed — rigid, will be violated within weeks) vs "We agree after-hours messages don't require a response until next business day; if something is genuinely urgent, we call" (co-created — flexible, includes an escalation path). The co-created versions are deliberately longer; that specificity IS the mechanism.

InteractionDrag-and-drop sorting with pair-comparison feedback
M3-07 When the Pressure Returns ▾
Learning Purpose

The hardest test of sustainable norms: a genuine deadline crunch. The way a manager behaves during pressure defines the norm — not how they behave when things are calm.

Scenario

Quarter-end. 10 days to a critical client demo. Two unresolved bugs. Karan is stretched. VP says: "I need your team at full capacity until this ships." Options: rally everyone and cancel focus days; triage privately and protect the rest of the team; push back on the VP by naming the tradeoff.

Design Note

Options B and C are both defensible — B absorbs pressure personally, C manages upward. This reflects reality: sustainable management has multiple valid approaches. The VP isn't a villain; the deadline is genuine; the pressure is legitimate.

InteractionHigh-stakes branching — course's emotional peak
M3-08 Rituals That Reinforce ▾
Learning Purpose

Give managers concrete, implementable rituals that reinforce sustainable norms without requiring constant willpower. Shift from "trying harder" to "building systems."

Five Rituals

Monday Capacity Check; Friday Shutdown Message (manager sends first); Monthly Norm Review; Recognition Reframe in retros; Manager Check-in Reversal (quarterly upward feedback invitation). Learner selects Implement / Adapt / Skip for each.

InteractionPlanning tool — not scored, downloadable summary
M3-09 Scenario Assessment ▾
Scenario

Three months post-course pulse survey results. Three metrics improved. One didn't move: "I believe our team agreements will hold during high-pressure periods" — unchanged at 2.9/5.

Q1

Which metric should concern you most? Correct: the unchanged 2.9 metric. "Your team has experienced the calm-weather version of your new norms. They haven't yet seen you protect them under pressure."

Q2

Most effective next action? Correct: wait for the next high-pressure period and protect the norms visibly. "Trust is built through demonstrated behaviour during stress, not through announcements or workshops."

InteractionTwo-part knowledge check — primary LMS reporting point
M3-10 Your 30-Day Commitment ▾
Learning Purpose

Close the course. Frame the next 30 days as the real test — not the course itself. Leave the learner with a specific, personal, and actionable commitment framework.

30-Day Framework

Weeks 1–2: choose one pattern from Module 1 and change it visibly. Weeks 2–3: have one co-creation conversation with the team using the agreement pattern. Weeks 3–4: the next time pressure rises, protect the norm. Don't explain why. Just do it.

Developer Notes

Do NOT add a completion celebration screen. No confetti, no badge, no "Congratulations!" The course ends the way it began: with seriousness and respect for the problem. If the LMS requires a completion page: "Course complete. Your 30-day commitment starts now."
